Our Robot

Current Capabilities

Our robot is primarily designed towards the current season of the First® Tech Challenge: INTO THE DEEPSM presented by RTX. The main scoring element for this competition is the sample, which is a rectangular prism with triangular indents. We currently use the goBilda Strafer Chassis V4 kit as the base of our robot, which includes goBilda Mecanum wheels that allow movement in any direction. We plan for our robot to feature an efficient intake system to gather and transfer the samples to the deposit system. More details can be found in Goals. Once our sample is gathered, a colour sensor detects its colour. If it identifies the wrong colour, the intake will automatically release the sample.

Next, we have two goBilda servos that raise our arm up and back into the robot. This is where our deposit system comes into play, consisting of two 4-stage Viper-Slides from goBilda, along with our custom-designed claw. Once the intake system brings the sample into the robot, the claw grabs onto it, and the driver maneuvers to the target bucket. The driver then presses the deposit button, triggering an automated command to deposit the sample. When this occurs, the two Viper-Slides extend and the intake spins in the opposite direction to prevent jams. After the sample is released from the intake, the claw will align with the high basket while the driver positions the robot to ensure the sample properly lands into the basket. Finally, the driver presses the button to open the claw and release the sample, later pressing the same button to retract everything back into the robot. This is a quick overview of our robot and how it operates efficiently and effectively to score points in this year's game.

Goals

Even with the many mechanisms our robot already contains, there is still much to be done. We have prototyped an “Active Intake” that utilizes a soft, rubber wheel with spikes, spinning inward to gather the samples. This wheel will be powered by a goBilda 5203 series motor, capable of spinning at up to 1620 RPM. Using this design will allow us to achieve the efficient and effective intake system which we initially envisioned. The claw also needs a way to be controlled. A design for a differential wrist is in progress which, if implemented, will allow 2-axes of 360-degree motion. This will provide the precise transfer and deposit system that we need. Another option is a coaxial virtual 4-bar. This option would have a similar range of motion and therefore similar benefits. We are currently debating on current designs due to their individual pros and cons. Finally, our drop-down arms may work for now, yet they have room for improvement. If funding and time allows, we plan to replace them for small linear slides, increasing efficiency in the intake system. These upgrades can be quite costly, requiring proper management of funds. More details can be found on Funding.
